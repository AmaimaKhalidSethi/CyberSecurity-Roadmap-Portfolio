Comparing incidents across multiple dimensions reveals their core nature. While NotPetya dominated in technical destruction, the Twitter hack highlighted a massive failure in human-centric security controls.

1 NotPetya: The Destructive Wiper

Originally mistaken for ransomware, NotPetya was a state-sponsored weapon targeting the Ukrainian financial supply chain. It utilized a "Trojanized" software update to enter networks and **EternalBlue** to propagate laterally at machine speed.

Vector

Compromised M.E.Doc Update Server (Supply Chain Attack).

Payload

Overwrites MBR/MFT; Encryption is permanent with no recovery math.

2 The Twitter Vishing Funnel

The 2020 Twitter breach proved that MFA is not a silver bullet if the human component is compromised. Attackers used "Vishing" (Voice Phishing) and a reverse-proxy phishing portal to intercept credentials and session tokens in real-time.

01
Reconnaissance

OSINT on LinkedIn to map internal hierarchies and IT support staff.

02
The Call

Vishing remote employees posing as helpdesk troubleshooting VPN issues.

03
Credential Theft

Spoofed SSO portal intercepts AuthN credentials and active MFA tokens.

04
Admin Abuse

Unauthorized access to Internal Tools allowed email overrides on VIP accounts.

3 Dyn DDoS: The Mirai Storm

The 2016 attack on Dyn DNS exploited over 100,000 insecure IoT devices (cameras, DVRs) with factory-default passwords. This "Mirai" botnet paralyzed global services like Netflix and Reddit by flooding DNS resolvers with junk traffic.

  • Brute Force: Targeted Telnet ports 23/2323.
  • Vector: Only 61 hardcoded default password pairs.
  • Result: 1.2 Terabits per second of pure unauthenticated volume.
